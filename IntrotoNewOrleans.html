So...

In 1682, a French Explorer name Lasalle found a river the natives called the "Mississippi," or "Big River." He travels all the way down it to the Gulf of Mexio and realizes he found a super highway through the continent. Naturally, he claims that land for France and names it in honor of the current French King, Louis XIV. He calls it Louis' Land or, Louisana.

Lasalle goes back to France and says "Louis, I found this big piece of land in the New World and named it for you. Let's colonize it." So, they send a second expledition out in 1699 led by two brothers, the Le Moyne brothers. The older is named Iberville and the younger is named Bienville (he is more important to us).

They come up through the Gulf of Mexico and stop in Mobile Bay. Mobile, Al is the first capital of French Louisana. Shortly after, they find the mouth of the river and start sending expeditions up river. When they get to a particular bend in the river, Bienville says, "This is a Beautiful Crescent on the river, let's stop here." However, Iberville was the governor and didn't want to stop, so they continued further up until Iberville found a spot he liked. In order to remember where it was when he came back, Iberville stuck a big red stick in the ground on the shore That is now Baton Rouge, or "Red Stick," LA.

Iberville dies, Bienville becomes the new governor and in the year...

1718

Jean-Baptiste Le Moyne de Bienville returns to the Beautiful Crescent on the river and founds the settlement of Nouvelle-Orléans. In 1718, Louis XIV was dead and his son, Louis XV was only eight years old. So, their cousin by marriage, Phillipe II, Duke of Orléans was the Regent of France until Louis XV was an adult. We are named for Phillipe II, Duke of Orléans.

The following year in 1719, the first two slave ships arrive carrying roughyl 250 slaves a piece. That nearly doubles the population in a single day. Their labor, the labor of their decendents, and the other slaves transported during the Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade will build nearly everything in New Orleans before 1863 and emancipation.

We are French from the years 1718 through 1762. In 1762, Louis XV is an adult, going over the books for the French crown and he realizes the French crown is in a pretty tight spot, financially. There were a big list of problems. Two of the more important ones to us:

  1. The Louisana Territory was costing more money to maintain than what they received back from it.
  2. They were about to lose a war with the British. In America, we call it the French and Indian War, but it is really a part of the larger Seven Years War.

So, Louis XV approaches his first cousin, Carlos III of Spain. He says, "Hey Chuckie, I got a piece of land in the New World and I don't want it anymore. If you aid me against the British, I will give you the Louisana Territory." Carlos says, "That's a great deal."

So, in 1762, France and Spain sign the secret Treaty of Fountainbleau, handing control of the Territory from the French to the Spanish. But they never told the citizens of New Orleans they were now Spanish until 1763 when, surprise, a new Spanish government appears on the river. And, the Frenchmen who already lived there, didn't take kindly to the new Spanish government.

So, they rebel against the Spanish by sending ambassadors to Louis XV declaring how terrible Spanish rule is, begging to be taken back. Louis XV upholds the Spanish rule and sends them packing. Fed up with colonial rule, the Frenchmen turn to violence and forcebly kick the Spanish governor, Antonio de Ulloa, out of Louisana, back to Spain.

In a response, the Spanish sent a new governor. This time, he was an Irish guy; a mercenary in the Spanish army. His name was Alejandro O'Reilly. When O'Reilly arrives he says, "Hey guys, I've just imported some wine. Let's drink it and we can calmly discuss Spanish colonializm." Well, 5 frenchmen arrive for dinner and drinks. They are promptly shot. Their heads are cut off, left on spikes at the downriver boundry of the settlement where Frenchmen St is today. Today on Frenchmen St, you can listen to music seven nights a week.

So, we are now a Spanish colony from 1762 through 1800. During that time, the city burns down twice in 1788 and 1794. Because of the two fires, the Spanish's thoughtful and dilligent reaction to rebuilding the city, and their influence during the reconstruction, we call the architecture in the French Quarter Spanish Caribbean, not French Colonial.

Meanwhile, on July 14, 1789, La Fete de la Bastille, the people storm the bastille and arm themselves. In the middle of The French Revolution, Louis XVI and Marie Antoinette lose their heads. Beheading the Monarchy was a very revolutionary thing to do. So, this guy named Napoleon takes over France.

In 1800, Napoleon is working on conquering all of Europe to establish what he will later call The First French Empire. When he becomes knocking on the door for Spain, the Spanish government says to him, "Don't invade us and we'll give you the Louisana Territory." Napoleon takes that deal. However, because of the Hatian Revolution, Napoleon had no solid foothold in the new world and couldn't care less about New Orleans. So, for three years from 1800 through 1803, the colony is owned by France, but still controlled by the Spanish government already in place.

That is confusing. Let's simplify things. This all leads us to the year 1803.

Thomas Jefferson, the third American president, wants to buy the City of New Orleans from Napoleon. That is all he cares about; he only wants New Orleans. Because, if you control New Orleans, you control the entire Mississippi River. The Mississippi is the longest river on the North American continent. It is the fourth longest river in the entire world. In 1803, there are no planes, trains or automobiles. Everyone travels and every good is shipped on the water.

So, Napoleon says to Thomas Jefferson, "Ok, sure I'll sell you New Orleans. But, you have to take the entire Louisiana Territory in the deal. You can't have New Orleans all by itself." Jefferson goes, "Ok, but, there's nothing in the territory, yeah? So it's only worth $15 million."

The Louisana Territory was much larger than the state of Louisana is today. It stretched from the Gulf of Mexico into present day Canada. From the Mississippi River to the Rocky Mountaints. It was roughly 828,000 square miles. At $15 million, that was $18 for a square mile. However, that's in 1803 dollars, which is misleading to leave it at that. When you do the adjustment, that $15 million is closer to $250 million. That is still less than $300 for a square mile. Today all that land is valued at $1.4 trillion. It's still considered the greatest real estate deal in history. Eventually fifteen states were carved out of that land purchased.

Finally, in 1812, Louisana became the eighteenth state in the Union.
